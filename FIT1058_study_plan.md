## How each day works (~2–3 h)

Use the same rhythm every day so it becomes automatic:

1. **Warm-up (15 min):** redo 2–3 problems from a *previous* weak topic (spaced repetition). This is built into the checklists below.
2. **Learn/review (60–90 min):** read the relevant Course Notes sections, work the worked examples by hand, summarise key rules onto a one-page "cheat sheet" for that topic.
3. **Practice (45–60 min):** do that day's practice set (ask me to generate it — "give me today's practice").
4. **Error log (10 min):** write every mistake into `error_log.md` with the correct method. Re-read this file before the exam — it's your highest-value revision.

**Exam-format note:** the paper mixes single-answer MCQs, select-all questions, and **written proofs**. Because proofs are a weak spot and they're worth big marks, practise *writing full proofs by hand*, not just recognising the idea.

## Exam-day strategy (Mon 15 Jun)

- **Triage:** in the first 2 minutes, skim the whole paper and do the MCQs/short answers first to bank easy marks, then return to the multi-mark proofs.
- **Budget time by marks:** roughly 1.5–2 min per mark in a 3-hour paper. Don't sink 30 min into a 4-mark proof.
- **Proofs:** state your method ("By induction…", "Assume for contradiction…"), define variables, and justify each line — partial structure earns marks even if you stall.
- **Show working** on number theory and counting — method marks matter.
- **Never leave MCQs blank** — eliminate options and guess.
